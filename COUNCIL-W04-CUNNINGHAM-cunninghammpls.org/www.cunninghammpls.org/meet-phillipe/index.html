Meet Phillipe Cunningham.

Phillipe Cunningham grew up in the cornfields of Illinois. He was raised by a 40-year United Autoworkers Association member and a blue-collar laborer. One of his earliest memories from childhood is at just five years old standing next to his father on the picket line during a contract strike. Standing up for just treatment of workers at such a young age instilled in Phillipe a commitment to making his community a better place. Phillipe grew to become a civilly-minded youth leader engaged in service and leadership with impact far beyond the boundaries of his rural town.

 

Youth Work

While attending college in Oakland, CA, youth work became Phillipe’s central career focus. He set a goal to become a teacher because he realized that working with youth is a very effective way to make positive social change in the world. Phillipe moved to Chicago to finish college at DePaul University where upon graduation, he was accepted into a prestigious teaching fellowship, Chicago Teaching Fellows. Phillipe became a special education teacher who specialized as a behavioral specialist in Chicago Public Schools. He taught young people with Autism Spectrum Disorders on the Southside of Chicago and was a proud and very active Chicago Teachers Union, Local 1 member. In addition to being a classroom teacher, Phillipe was a pro-bono advocate for low-income parents whose children were in need of special education services and/or facing unfair punishment in zero tolerance school discipline codes.

 

Moving to Minneapolis

When he put his roots down in North Minneapolis, Phillipe brought with him the lessons he learned as a youth worker and community advocate and began organizing high school students to engage in the public policy process. He supported a group of students at the Capitol to advocate for state legislation that would provide funding for afterschool programs for students across Minnesota.

 

Serving at City Hall

That same year, Phillipe became involved with City government and was appointed by Mayor Betsy Hodges to serve on the Youth Violence Prevention Executive Committee. With an expertise in youth policy and youth development practices, Phillipe brought a fresh energy and perspective to the committee as a young youth worker. On this City of Minneapolis committee, Phillipe was a vocal advocate for Northside youth and the community. He passionately advocated for sustainable, 21st century youth violence prevention practices based on research-proven public health and youth development strategies. Phillipe’s advocacy for the North Side on this committee set him apart. Within a year, Mayor Hodges recruited Phillipe to join her administration.

 

Expertise and Call to Action

Phillipe brings to the City of Minneapolis valuable knowledge and expertise in youth development and racial equity policy, in addition to his experiences with direct service and advocacy work in the community. As Mayor Hodges’ senior policy advisor, his policy portfolio included K-12 public education, higher education, youth success, racial equity, and LGBTQ rights. In this role, he remained a steadfast advocate for the Northside by engaging in community work and advising policies that have impacted North Minneapolis in particular, in addition to the rest of the City. Phillipe is the primary coordinator for the local efforts of President Obama’s My Brother’s Keeper Initiative, which focuses on improving the life outcomes of boys and young men of color. For more information about his work throughout the Northside, please check out the “My Love for the Northside” video under the “Issues” heading.

 

Other Facts

Outside of his work in the Mayor’s Office, he is a member of the University of Minnesota’s National Diversity Advisory Board, which helps advise the UMN system on their equity and diversity efforts, policies and practices. He volunteers weekly at Youthlink as a co-facilitator of a support group for LGBTQ homeless youth. He also is a trainer and consultant for diversity and inclusion. Phillipe is currently in the last phase of completing his Master’s in Education in Youth Development Leadership at the University of Minnesota, Twin Cities.

Phillipe, his husband Lane, and their two dogs and two cats live in the McKinley neighborhood and are active in their block club.
